Chemistry: Alloys

On the periodic table, there are about 91 metals. However, even though there are quite a lot of them, there are still more metals that we need to use. Consider brass, this is used to make quite a lot of the parts of a saxophone and yet, no matter how hard you look, you won't find it on the periodic table. Why? Because the metals on the periodic table are malleable - so they can easily be shaped however you want it. This isn't a good thing, especially in saxophones, as the sound that comes out of a saxophone may be distorted - just by holding it. So, in order to make saxophones more durable, we make them out of alloys. But how? Let's find out...

How can we make alloys?
Alloys are made by melting multiple mettals and then puttung together into one giant lump of metal. For instance, to make the brass in a saxophone, you need to melt copper and zinc (both elements found on the periodic table) together.

But it's not that easy. Copper and zinc both have really high melting points (copper: 1,085°C, zinc: ~420°C). And then once you melt those, you need to mix them together in a specific ratio (~70% copper, and ~30% zinc) to form the perfect brass. Trust me, it's really difficult.

(Side note: If you want to make brass at home, seek parent's permission and also try not to burn yourself, or melt yourself...)
